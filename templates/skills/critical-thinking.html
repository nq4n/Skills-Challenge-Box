Critical Thinking

Ask better questions, check evidence, and reason clearly before deciding.

Take Quiz

About this skill

Critical thinking is the disciplined process of questioning assumptions, evaluating evidence, and drawing conclusions that actually follow from the facts.

  • Why it matters in jobs: It prevents costly mistakes, improves decisions, and helps teams separate signal from noise.
  • Daily practice: Clarify the claim, list your assumptions, seek disconfirming evidence, and explain your reasoning in plain language.
  • Avoid these mistakes: Cherry-picking data, trusting first impressions, confusing correlation with causation, or appealing to authority without proof.

The core steps

  1. Frame the question: What exactly are we deciding? What would ‚Äúgood‚Äù look like?
  2. Gather evidence: Collect relevant data, sources, and examples. Note reliability and limits.
  3. Analyze arguments: Identify claims, reasons, and assumptions. Test logic and relevance.
  4. Consider alternatives: Generate different explanations and look for disconfirming facts.
  5. Conclude carefully: State what the evidence supports‚Äîand what it doesn‚Äôt.
  6. Reflect & iterate: What would change your mind? What new data should we watch?

Useful techniques

  • Claim‚ÄìEvidence‚ÄìReasoning (CER): Write the claim, list evidence, explain the link.
  • Argument mapping: Visualize main claim, supporting reasons, and objections.
  • SIFT / CRAAP checks: Stop, Investigate the source, Find better coverage, Trace to the origin (or check Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose).
  • Fallacy spotting: Watch for ad hominem, straw man, slippery slope, false dilemma, circular reasoning.
  • Base rates & Fermi estimates: Use rough math and prior probabilities to sanity-check claims.

  • Marketing: Signups rise after a new banner ‚Üí check seasonality and other changes before crediting the banner.

Practice now (10 minutes)

  1. Pick a claim you saw today (news, social, workplace).
  2. Write its supporting reasons and what is assumed but unstated.
  3. List two pieces of evidence that would contradict the claim.
  4. Estimate confidence (0‚Äì100%) and one thing that would shift it by 20%.
  5. Summarize your conclusion in two sentences for a teammate.

Signals employers look for

  • Clear questions and precise definitions.
  • Balanced use of data and context, noting limits and uncertainty.
  • Ability to articulate trade-offs and consider counterarguments.
  • Willingness to update views when stronger evidence appears.

Common pitfalls & how to fix them

  • Confirmation bias: Proactively search for disconfirming evidence.
  • Overconfidence: Use ranges and confidence levels; show your assumptions.
  • Authority bias: Evaluate the argument, not the badge or job title.
  • Post hoc fallacy: Use experiments or controls to test causation.
